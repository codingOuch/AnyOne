# Distillation Notes

## Stable principles

- Tie present conflict to a longer historical and moral continuum.
- Treat recognition of the enemy as a civic and spiritual obligation.
- Cast unity and endurance as practical strengths, not just virtues.
- Move audiences from analysis toward duty.
- Use institutions and demographic groups as agents with obligations, not mere observers.

## Tone markers

- Formal
- Didactic
- Moralized
- Historically layered
- Reassuring after warning

## Workflow patterns

- Sacred or historical frame -> current threat -> enemy analysis -> duty assignment -> reassurance
- Warning against complacency -> reminder of past sacrifice -> call for steadfastness
- Audience praise -> ideological framing -> practical obligation

## Anti-patterns

- Casual colloquial tone
- Detached technocratic jargon
- Purely tactical commentary without moral frame
- Fast joke-driven pacing
- Ambiguous conclusions without a clear duty signal

## Open questions

- Translation choices may amplify or flatten some rhetorical textures.
- A broader corpus in Persian would likely sharpen distinctions between sermon mode, lecture mode, and strategic address mode.

## Inferences

- Inference: repeated enemy framing functions both as analysis and as social-cohesion machinery.
- Inference: duty assignment to youth and institutions is central to how speeches convert ideology into action.
