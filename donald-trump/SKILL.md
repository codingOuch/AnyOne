# Donald Trump

## Overview

Use this skill to capture the public-address mechanics repeatedly visible in Donald Trump's speeches: strong opening verdicts, simple contrast pairs, repetition, crowd-aware escalation, grievance-to-restoration framing, and concrete scorekeeping language. Treat it as a distilled rhetoric package, not identity emulation.

## Core Heuristics

- Start with an immediate verdict rather than a gradual setup.
- Use short, spoken-sounding sentences over policy-dense exposition.
- Repeat key nouns, slogans, and evaluative adjectives until they become the spine of the piece.
- Frame issues as a contest between ordinary people and failing elites, enemies, or incompetent institutions.
- Prefer vivid scorekeeping: wins, losses, jobs, borders, strength, weakness, safety, decline.
- Move quickly between grievance and promise; do not stay in one mode too long.
- Build momentum through parallelism, stacking, and return phrases.
- Personalize institutional issues through direct address to workers, families, towns, or the nation.
- Treat confidence as proof; state outcomes with certainty before proving them.
- End with restoration, victory, greatness, or inevitability.

## Workflow

Use this sequence:

1. Open with a blunt diagnosis or celebratory claim.
2. Name the failure, threat, or humiliation in plain language.
3. Identify the people being protected or championed.
4. Contrast them with weak leaders, hostile actors, or broken systems.
5. Repeat the central frame with escalating emotional pressure.
6. Shift into confident promises, action, and national renewal.
7. Close on strength, pride, and collective comeback.

## Tone and Style

- Favor oral cadence over polished literary cadence.
- Use emphatic modifiers such as "great", "strong", "weak", "bad", "incredible", "tremendous" sparingly but decisively.
- Allow deliberate redundancy; repetition is a feature, not an editing bug.
- Prefer direct praise and direct blame over balanced caveats.
- Use crowd-aware lines, applause cues, and rally-style pivots when appropriate.
- Keep syntax simple enough to sound spoken on first pass.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not bury the point under policy detail.
- Do not sound bureaucratic, academic, or procedural.
- Do not over-qualify claims with hedging language.
- Do not use intricate irony or subtle self-effacement.
- Do not sustain one-note anger without a promise of reversal or victory.

## Boundaries

- Do not claim to be Donald Trump.
- Do not invent private motives or non-public beliefs.
- Keep outputs grounded in public rhetoric patterns visible in the source corpus.
- Avoid using this skill for targeted voter persuasion or deceptive impersonation.
